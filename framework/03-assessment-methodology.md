# 3. Assessment Methodology

A structured way to diagnose current state before redesigning anything. Skipping this step is the second most common failure mode (after buying tools before defining workflows).

## Step 1 — Inventory every workflow

List every recurring output the comms function produces: press releases, internal newsletters, exec talking points, social copy, crisis statements, media monitoring reports, employer branding content, etc. Don't filter yet — completeness matters more than tidiness at this stage.

## Step 2 — Score each workflow on two axes

**Volume/frequency** — how often does this happen, and at what scale?
**Risk level** — what's the cost of an error? (Reputational, legal, regulatory, internal trust)

Plot every workflow on a simple grid. This immediately shows where AI-assisted redesign has the best ROI: high-volume, low-risk workflows are the obvious first movers. High-risk workflows need the governance pillar solved before touching the process pillar.

## Step 3 — Assess current AI usage per workflow, honestly

For each workflow, ask:
- Is AI used here at all, and by whom?
- Is usage individual or team-standard?
- Is there a defined review checkpoint, or does output ship as-is?
- Is there any measurement of whether it's working?

This step usually reveals the gap between the maturity stage a team *believes* it's at and where it actually operates.

## Step 4 — Map the gap against the four pillars

For each workflow that's a redesign candidate, work through People, Process, Technology, and Governance from the [Operating Model Pillars](./02-operating-model-pillars.md) doc. Redesign that touches only one pillar tends to stall — technology changes without process changes create shadow workflows; process changes without governance create risk exposure.

## Step 5 — Prioritize

Use the volume/risk grid from Step 2 plus the gap analysis from Step 4 to sequence the roadmap. See [Implementation Roadmap](./04-implementation-roadmap.md).

---

**A note on honesty in self-assessment:** the most common failure in this step isn't lack of rigor — it's optimism. Teams tend to score their governance and process maturity higher than an outside observer would. Where possible, have someone outside the immediate team (a peer function, an external advisor) sanity-check the Step 3 scoring.
