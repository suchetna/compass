# 2. The Four Pillars of an AI-Native Comms Operating Model

Redesigning around AI means touching all four. Teams that redesign only one (usually technology) stay stuck at Stage 1 or 2.

## Pillar 1 — People

What changes:
- Roles shift from *producing* content to *directing and validating* it — the editorial judgment moves upstream
- New capability required: prompt design, output evaluation, and knowing when *not* to trust the model
- Career paths need a new rung between "junior writer" and "senior strategist" — someone fluent in AI-assisted production at scale
- Team composition shifts: fewer pure output roles, more roles that combine subject expertise with AI fluency

Questions to answer:
- Which current roles absorb AI capability, and which roles does it make redundant?
- Who on the team is actually equipped to evaluate AI output critically, not just generate it?

## Pillar 2 — Process

What changes:
- Workflows need explicit stage-gates: where AI drafts, where a human edits, where a human approves
- Review checkpoints shift from "check everything" to risk-weighted review — high-stakes output (crisis statements, exec communications) gets more human scrutiny than routine output (internal FAQs, social scheduling)
- Version control and audit trails become necessary, not optional, once AI is producing at volume
- Feedback loops need to exist: is the AI output getting *better* over time because the team is refining prompts and standards, or just faster?

Questions to answer:
- For each major workflow, what's the minimum viable human checkpoint?
- Where does speed currently override quality control, and is that a deliberate choice?

## Pillar 3 — Technology

What changes:
- Tool selection follows workflow redesign, not the other way around — buying a platform before defining the workflow is the most common failure mode
- Integration matters more than capability — a powerful tool that doesn't fit existing systems (CMS, intranet, approval chains) creates shadow workflows
- Data hygiene becomes a comms function responsibility, not just IT's — AI output quality depends on the quality of what it's trained or grounded on
- Build vs. buy decisions should map to how differentiated a workflow is — commodity tasks (transcription, summarization) favor buying; brand-voice-critical tasks may favor custom tooling

Questions to answer:
- Does your current toolset match your actual highest-volume workflows, or was it chosen for a different reason (vendor relationship, executive preference)?
- What's your data governance posture for anything fed into an AI system?

## Pillar 4 — Governance

What changes:
- Governance shifts from a compliance checklist to a design principle built into the workflow itself
- Risk tiers need defining: what counts as low-risk (internal newsletter draft) versus high-risk (regulatory disclosure, crisis response, anything with legal exposure)
- Explainability becomes a real requirement, not a nice-to-have — especially where AI-assisted decisions affect regulated communications or public-facing claims
- Accountability needs to be explicit: if AI-assisted content causes a problem, who owns the decision that let it ship?

Questions to answer:
- Do you have a written, workflow-specific policy for human-in-the-loop requirements, or an informal norm?
- Who is accountable when AI-assisted output causes an error — and does that person know it?
